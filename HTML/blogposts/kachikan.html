価値観

Blatantly self indulgent, but too many people ask so why not write it down.

The title of the post is 価値観kachikan only really because I think it sounds nicer than "sense of values". But that is what this post is about. I want to properly write out how I feel about certain things. Thanks random weird man on fedi for inspiring me to write this post.

Influenced by my family, I had a teenagehood blindly supporting Høyre, a Norwegian political party often badly called "The Conservative Party" in English. They're definitely that for Norway, but I feel it gives a wrong impression these days. They're closer to like blue dog Democrats, or British One Nation Conservatives. A somewhat dated worldview of progressive conservatism that is artificially kept afloat in Norway and its particularly bizarre political landscape.

While I was for a short while roped into more right wing fringes through everyone's favourite youtube algorithm, by the time I was 13-14 I was decidedly a standard issue progressive. Though still sympathetic to the sort of views that my family had. After that, the story of what drove me to actually genuinelly explore politics is, in my mind, quite entertaining.

Due to the nature of my father's work, he got acquainted with a lot of rich people, and since I was 15 they didn't let me stay at home alone, so I was dragged along to a 50 year old birthday celebration in Provence. Quite unremarkable, it was essentially just a vacation. However, all the other people there, were more like my father's friend than my own family. All the other teens there were rich kids. It was my inability to properly relate to them at all that made something loosen up in my mind. This explicit and acute sense of alienation from people I thought were supposed to be "just like me" fundamentally shifted my view.

It was that vacation I would start my long traversal into leftism, bouncing between many lables, ideology shopping one may say. In my "ideology shopping era", Marxism-Leninism was definitely what I settled on the longest, and it wasn't until 2024 it actually started shifting as I started interacting with Left-Communists, specifically of the Italian variety.

The reason Left-communism was different to me was because it finally felt like I was among a group that properly encouraged reading, discussion and reflection. No longer was it "Read Blackshirts & Reds" or some of those god-awful Stalin writeups, then no further reflection. There was genuine participation in discourse and truly making sure you get those works. For this very reason, despite my present disagreements with Italian Left-Communists, I appreciate that their spaces, toxic as they may have been, encouraged me to engage with political and philosophical discourse in a different way.

As of present I have no explicit label on my politics. And that is very intentional. It's not that I'm a political vagabond, I feel quite comfortable in my outlook. That's not to say that I somehow have it all solved or that I cannot learn, but rather I feel I have a much more consistent outlook and process, not something that gets overturned by the latest good rhetoric I heard.

The inspiration of this post called me a coward for refusing a political label, but I don't see it as cowardice, rather humbleness. I am admitting to both myself and others that I am in no position to prescribe a theory of this world, or suggest an improvement. It's a bit of a cliché but it truly is a case of, the more you know, the more you realise you don't know. And while only tangentially related. I am increasingly valuing humbleness as a trait as of late.

In the extremely overquoted passage on religion by Marx. I've over time become more and more fond of the part: "But man is no abstract being squatting outside the world. Man is the world of man—state, society. This state and this society produce religion, which is an inverted conciousness of the world, because they are an inverted world."

I think this is goes beyond just religion. But rather ideology as a whole. We are no abstract beings, on a whim emancipating ourselves from the society that we are born in and forced to be within. We cannot simply say we do not believe in such social constructs as money, parliaments and race and make ourselves free from their infinitely reaching tentacles that attach themselves to every social interaction we partake in. Religion is in essence just an ideology in and of itself. There is at the end of the day, nothing fundamentally different between a Catholic and an enlightenment Liberal.

Any political stance I ascribe to myself, inherently gets tainted by the society I live within. My "desires" of an unknowable future hinges on what I do know. A serf could not imagine commuting to work by bullet train, not just because the concrete technology of "bullet train" doesn't exist, in fact that's the trivial part. A serf could probably easily imagine fast trains, or if we imagine a serf before the age of trains, the abstract concept of fast transit. What a serf could not imagine however would be the fabric of society. The "commuting" and "work" parts. Just as a serf could only imagine a better life as either a free peasant, or their own lord. Me, a mere proletariat, can only imagine regressive self sufficiency (the folly of Anarchists), or relying upon the labour of others, be it through bourgeois means or collectivism (incl. welfare).

As an aside, this is exactly the reason I am not a leftist. Leftists seek to emancipate the proletariat through collectivism, but both proletariat and collectivism only make sense as terms within class society. Essentially reifying class society despite claiming to be against it.

My vision of politics has become what some call nihilism. Though I reject that label. It's not that I don't believe in change, or see no point in wishing for change. It's more that I see chance as out of our hands, and that the change that is to occur will be something we cannot predict. While I disagree on many things. I do in fact quite enjoy how Marx makes very few actual predictions of communist society. Future society can only be defined by what it is not. In the rawest sense I am a Communist for I wish for such a society, but I disagree with too many positions of political strategy that communists hold, in addition to being anti-revolutionist, and as such, I do not take on the label.

But what about the present? I'm clearly passionate about many things. And that is true, I can get into quite heated discussions about the nature of the world, in addition to arguing against bigotry. I have been accused as holding a contradictory position of both rejecting bringing about change, yet seemingly advocating for change. The simple answer is that I am not advocating for change in the sense of the underlying social fabric. Merely trying to control the tides around me.

I do not wish to be around bigotry or unpleasantness. And so I do my best to clean my environment of it. Where I differ from many people who speak of "fighting against bigotry" is that I don't see it as a service to a grander cause, and as such, don't moralise participation in it. Arguing and harassing transphobes out of your social space is done purely for personal comfort for me and those I care for, it's not in the pursuit of a grander political aim. I wish there was a practical way to eradicate bigotry through mere individual actions, but sadly I cannot hold faith in such a reality.

To zoom out, this mindset extends to welfare and political rights. I am an advocate of a higher minimum wage, lower working hours, guaranteed vacation, &c. Not as a way to achieve something grander, but merely for comfort. Marx seeing the increasing political power of workers as the death throes of capital has been perverted by contemporary leftits to become a belief that reformism becomes the main vehicle for driving a revolutionary transformation of society. And that the ultimate goal becomes welfareism itself, as welfareism is the only way to progress.

And yes, reformism includes all of your favourite political revolutions. Even though they styled themselves as the executioners of capitalism, the Bolsheviks were merely Social Democrats without the democracy, demanding their welfare with rifles instead of ballots.

I must unfortunately disappoint in that I cannot properly elaborate on my views of democracy. It's extremely hard to describe in words. I can put in the fact that I have zero faith in democracy (NOT electoralism, democracy itself), but with the important caveat that that doesn't come from imagining the average voter as untrustworthy or inefficient, with some weird glassy eyed idealism of dictatorships being "more efficient".

It's important to be explicit about the fact that I do not view myself beyond ideology, as previously talked about, I recognise that any vision I have of the future is inherently tainted by the present. One cannot merely recognise ideology and then declare oneself emancipated from it. Nor can one truly disarm it in their thoughts. I am sure my thoughts are filled with many holes, leaps of logic and magical thinking, as are the thoughts of all humans.

Of course, this is all just my opinion.

Made with spite, hatred and excessive hubris.
