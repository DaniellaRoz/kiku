Hjertebarn

This post discusses physical health troubles and family issues, such as neglect.

In 2024, I was told "you cannot blame your heart anymore, you're just in bad shape". The following year, the doctor said my heart was "almost normal", and that I had no stenosis, with only prehypertension.

But why do I still feel ill?

When I close my eyes I hear the excessively loud ventilation of the examination room, the strange but familiar noises of the ultrasound playback and the humming sphygmomanometer. I vividly imagine the electrodes stuck to my body with its cumbersome wires. The adhesive pulling against my body hair, painfully ripping each off one by one. The gooey gel of the ultrasound, its weird smell and cold sensation the moment the transducer hits my chest. I remember the uncomfortable bench and the awkward grip over my half naked body. I remember my parents looking not at me, but at the screen, replaced by a girlfriend looking at only me, to no one, just an awkward chat with the doctor about university and my interests.

After the 20 longest minutes of my life, the doctor throws a towel at me and says "looks good, see you in a year," and leaves the room.

I too leave soon, not wanting to stay in that examination room any longer. I get dressed, only about half the gel properly cleaned off and start waiting for the 3 hour bus home in a waiting room filled with mostly old people, probably similarly done with life like I am.

When I was born, I was immediately taken out of my mother's arms. As was the norm back then, way more hospitals had maternity wards compared to now. The hospital I was born at was not able to operate on my condition, so one of the first things I did in my life was a several hour long ambulance ride. Followed 6 days later with heart surgery.

From as long as I remember, the label thrust upon me has been "hjertebarn". Norwegian, meaning literally "heart child", it is a term referring to people who have congenital heart defects or other heart issues from a young age. The surgery I had as a newborn did not heal me. It merely allowed me to live. But it wasn't a fun living at all.

Aortic stenosis is the term. A narrowing of the aortic valve. I have lived most of my life with abysmal stamina. Incapable of running up stairs, out of breath walking up the smallest of hills.

Due to being kept in the dark about the nature of my condition, a lot of things I had to piece together myself. My doctors did most of the talking to my parents in terms I did not understand, and my parents relayed it back in ways that did not help. My father sounded more like he was threatening me. Don't you dare get worse health. While my mum took the path of worry, however that only served to make me start worrying that if I were to get worse health or otherwise have a medical urgency, she'd not be able to handle the anxiety.

The combination of both threats and worry has been a disaster to how I view my own body and my own health. My whole body is in service to my parents. Despite turning 22 soon, I still feel like they own my body, that I owe it to be pretty for them, to be healthy for them. That anything I do to my body requires their consent. And this has leaked into more than just my body. When I was going to visit someone in the United States, as a 20 year old. I still phoned my mother, essentially asking her for permission. Her words of "you're an adult, you can do what you want" unsurprisingly did not undo the 2 decades of damage done to my psyche.

In fact, they reinforce it, though I want to believe it's unwittingly. When I had gone almost a year of no contact with my parents, I finally met my mother again. One of the first things she said to me was. Thank god you haven't changed, I was worried you do something drastic like dye your hair. I often talk about having conservative parents, hopefully that gives an idea of what degree.

Just last week I saw my GP and she recommended I start medicating my anxiety. I do want to, I have wanted explicitly this since last year, but despite that, I feel hesitant, because I feel my dad will have opinions about it. So it isn't to convince me, or even my doctor. It's to convince him. In fact, throughout my life it's primarily been my father causing a bad body image. Not that my mother hasn't contributed, she has, but my father is by far the worst. One day my mother confided in me that my father too has body image issues. As if that was supposed to make me feel better. It only ended up disgusting me, it's clear he uses me as an outlet to project his own anxieties and insecurities.

Thanks to the young age I had my first surgery, the scar isn't merely a slightly more sensitive line going down my body, the skin there has fought against my growing body, and now "cuts in". It's blatantly obvious as a surgery scar even if it's almost completely faded. And it, much to my chagrin makes my left breast look weird. Definitely does not help my body image as well. My second scar is more "normal" running down my chest. It's visible if I wear a V neck, as a result, I never wear V necks. I often see people finding scars attractive. I absolutely do not. They remind me of too much. Seeing them, touching them, it never brings me joy.

I remember when I was younger, I felt a hesitancy to be topless. Of course it's obvious to me now as gender dysphoria. But I think it was genuinely also related to my, at the time, single scar. The body image issues were there even as a young person, but there was also an element of shame, of a defective body.

In general I do not report when I am ill to my parents, ever. Only when it's so bad I cannot ignore it. A couple years back I had a flareup of uveitis. I was able to recognise it quickly since my father had also had it. But at every turn, even when my father too expressed concern that it might be uveitis. I tried to downplay it. I didn't want to admit to them that I had failed at taking care of myself. I didn't want to feel like I was worrying them again. Uveitis ended up being a miserable experience, sure, but the overwhelming suffering was the shame. The shame that I had failed to take care of my own body, despite uveitis being autoimmune and essentially uncontrollable. It got so bad that I started wearing sunglasses constantly, even indoors, because I didn't want to show my inflamed right eye to anyone.

I am often hesitant to share to people, particularly in person, my experience with heart disease. Because it inevitably ends up becoming a discussion of my parents.

Last year, just before Christmas, I was visiting my grandma. After she was widowed my father often pressures me into visiting her. I usually do for the sake of appeasing him, as my grandma is a very unpleasant person. This visit would end up etching itself into my mind as we start talking about one of my brothers, who suffers from cluster headaches and had a near fatal lung infection as a teenager that left him with permanent reduced lung capacity. Beyond being siblings I feel a certain sense of kinship with that brother. We both struggle with chronic pain and health in general.

My grandma however, seems to be ignorant of my suffering, after remarking about how much my brother suffered, she said. "Good thing you don't really suffer at all, I mean, your heart, but you had surgery, so it's fine."

I have often grappled with the question of what it means to be disabled. If we accept "able-bodied" and "disabled" as two seperate discrete things, then surely there must be a line one crosses to go from able-bodied to disabled. An undrawable line, but a line nontheless. And despite people not being able to draw this line, everyone has a general sense of what is what. And what especially complicates things is invisible disability.

I do not know if I consider myself disabled, I have crippling anxiety, and chronic pain, though neither truly preventing me from doing anything. However, where does my heart fit in? I am operated after all. It is "almost normal", the only real heart issue I have is prehypertension. Is that a disability? Am I trivialising what it means to be disabled when my almost normal heart is counted? To what extent am I merely pathologising the fact that I'm in bad shape. Is that a disability? Are disabilities only things that cosmic unluckiness has bestowed upon you, or do things you are perfectly capable of getting out of without medical intervention also count?

I'm not interested in nor able to answer these questions, but they constantly weigh on me. To me, every person fitter and more sound of mind than me is "able bodied" while everyone who to me clearly suffers more are "disabled". My mind, to my annoyance as carved out an exceptional case of able-bodiedness, Daniella Anastasia, who is neither.

Even the label "hjertebarn" alienates me. I am operated, do I deserve this label now? A label also used for children who won't see their 7th birthday, and to people requiring a machine to keep them alive. All I needed was some deft application of a scalpel. At every stage, I feel both a shame for being not healthy, yet also a shame for lumping myself together with people that I have convinced myself "suffer more" than me.

I had originally intended to write more on this topic, but for this post I am reaching my limit, perhaps more will come later. I will leave a closing thought:

The doctors are vague about if I need to have a third surgery later in life or not. But above all else, I want a surgery that makes me forget that I was ever called hjertebarn.

Made with spite, hatred and excessive hubris.

悪意、憎悪、そして過剰な自惚れで作られた。
